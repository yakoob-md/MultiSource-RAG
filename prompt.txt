### Introduction to the Partition Plan of 1947
The Partition Plan of 1947 was a proposal by the United Nations (UN) to divide the British Mandate for Palestine into separate Jewish and Arab states, with Jerusalem to be administered by the UN. As stated in [Source 2], the plan was an attempt to resolve the Arab-Israeli conflict and provide a solution to the competing claims of the Jewish and Arab populations in the region.

### Background to the Partition Plan
According to [Source 1], the British government had been responsible for administering Palestine under a League of Nations mandate since 1920. However, the British government found it increasingly difficult to manage the conflicting demands of the Jewish and Arab populations, and in 1947, they referred the question of Palestine's future to the UN. As mentioned in [Source 7], the UN Special Committee on Palestine (UNSCOP) was established to investigate the situation and make recommendations for the future of the region.

### Key Provisions of the Partition Plan
The Partition Plan of 1947 proposed the following:
1. **Separate Jewish and Arab states**: The plan called for the creation of separate Jewish and Arab states, with the Jewish state to be established on approximately 55% of the territory and the Arab state on approximately 42%. As stated in [Source 4], the Jewish state was to include the Negev Desert, the Jezreel Valley, and the coastal plain, while the Arab state was to include the West Bank, the Gaza Strip, and parts of the Galilee.
2. **Jerusalem to be administered by the UN**: The plan proposed that Jerusalem, which was considered a holy city by both Jews and Arabs, be administered by the UN as a separate entity, with the goal of maintaining its historic and cultural significance. As mentioned in [Source 10], the city was to be governed by a special international regime, with the UN responsible for its administration and security.
3. **Economic union**: The plan proposed the establishment of an economic union between the Jewish and Arab states, with the goal of promoting economic cooperation and development in the region. As stated in [Source 11], the economic union was to include a customs union, a currency union, and a joint economic council to coordinate economic policies.
4. **Protection of minority rights**: The plan called for the protection of minority rights in both states, including the right to freedom of speech, assembly, and worship. As mentioned in [Source 12], the plan also proposed the establishment of a commission to investigate and resolve complaints of human rights abuses.

### Reactions to the Partition Plan
The Partition Plan of 1947 was met with mixed reactions from the Jewish and Arab populations:
1. **Jewish reaction**: The Jewish population, led by the Jewish Agency, accepted the plan, seeing it as a step towards the establishment of a Jewish state. As stated in [Source 13], the Jewish Agency believed that the plan provided a basis for the creation of a Jewish state, even if it was not perfect.
2. **Arab reaction**: The Arab population, led by the Arab Higher Committee, rejected the plan, arguing that it did not provide for the creation of a single, unified Arab state and that the proposed borders were unfair. As mentioned in [Source 14], the Arab Higher Committee believed that the plan was a betrayal of Arab rights and interests in the region.

### Aftermath of the Partition Plan
The Partition Plan of 1947 was never implemented, as it was rejected by the Arab states and the Arab population in Palestine. As stated in [Source 15], the plan's failure to provide a solution to the Arab-Israeli conflict led to the outbreak of the 1948 Arab-Israeli War, which resulted in the establishment of the State of Israel and the displacement of hundreds of thousands of Palestinians.
